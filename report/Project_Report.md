## Introduction

This project report documents my work as a Student Digital Ambassador for the CSE0001 Digital Literacy course. The assignment required building a comprehensive Digital Literacy Portfolio across five modules — covering visual content creation, professional online presence, exploration of coding and collaboration platforms, professional email communication, and cybercrime awareness. Each task was designed to develop a specific digital competency essential for a modern engineering student. All work has been organised in a single GitHub repository, and this report provides detailed reflections on each task, the tools used, challenges faced, and key learnings. Through this project, I gained practical, hands-on experience with tools and platforms that I will continue to use throughout my four-year degree and beyond.

## Task 1 — Create a Digital Literacy Awareness Infographic

For Task 1, I created a one-page infographic titled "Digital Literacy 101: Your Essential Guide" using **Canva**, a free, browser-based graphic design platform. I chose Canva because of its intuitive drag-and-drop interface, extensive template library, and professional-quality output — even for someone with no prior design experience.

The infographic covers three core topics: (1) **what digital literacy means** — going beyond basic computer skills to encompass the ability to find, evaluate, create, and communicate information responsibly using digital technologies; (2) **essential digital tools for students** — GitHub for code collaboration, Google Workspace for cloud productivity, Canva for design, and LinkedIn for professional networking; and (3) **safe internet practices** — including using strong passwords, enabling two-factor authentication, recognising phishing attempts, and protecting one's digital footprint.

One thing I found particularly interesting was how much thought goes into visual hierarchy in design. Deciding what to emphasise, which colours convey trust and clarity (I used a blue-and-white palette), and how to organise information so that a reader can scan it in seconds — these are skills I had not considered before, but they are clearly part of being digitally literate.

## Task 4 — Draft a Professional Email & Etiquette Guide

For Task 4, I drafted two professional emails and created a social media etiquette checklist.

**Email 1** was a request from a student to a professor for an assignment submission extension. I structured it with a clear subject line ("Request for Extension on Digital Literacy Assignment Submission"), a respectful greeting, a concise explanation of the reason (family commitment requiring travel), an acknowledgement of the inconvenience, and a professional sign-off with full contact details. **Email 2** was an expression of interest in a summer internship, addressed to a company's HR coordinator. It included my academic background, relevant skills, enthusiasm for the role, and a formal closing.

I also created a **Social Media Do's and Don'ts** checklist with five items in each category. Key do's include thinking before posting, maintaining professional profiles, and verifying information before sharing. Key don'ts include sharing personal details publicly, engaging in cyberbullying, and posting impulsively under emotional distress.

One scenario that illustrates the importance of digital communication etiquette: a student once sent a casual, poorly written email to a professor requesting a grade review, using slang and no subject line. The professor, perceiving the lack of effort and respect, declined to review the request. Had the student used a proper subject line, respectful tone, and structured body, the outcome might have been entirely different. This example underscores that how you communicate online is just as important as what you communicate.

## Task 5 — Cybercrime Awareness Case Study & Prevention Guide

For Task 5, I researched **UPI/Online Payment Fraud** — a cybercrime type that is especially relevant to college students in India who frequently use apps like Google Pay, PhonePe, and Paytm for everyday transactions.

What surprised me most during my research was how devastatingly simple UPI fraud is to execute. Unlike traditional hacking or phishing that requires some technical sophistication, UPI fraud often relies on nothing more than a phone call and social engineering. The scammer sends a "collect request" (which is actually a payment demand) and convinces the victim that accepting it will credit money to their account. The moment the victim enters their UPI PIN, the money is gone. The fact that you never need to enter a PIN to receive money is something I did not fully understand before this task — and I suspect many of my batchmates do not know this either.

As a result of this research, one habit I will personally change is: **I will never enter my UPI PIN in response to a collect request from an unknown person**, regardless of what they claim over the phone. I will also spread awareness about this among my family members, especially those who recently started using digital payments and may be more vulnerable.

I also created a comprehensive **Stay Safe Online** prevention checklist with 10 actionable tips, including two specifically about UPI/financial safety, and included the emergency reporting channels — the **National Cyber Crime Portal** (cybercrime.gov.in) and the **24×7 Helpline 1930**.
